Irish Property Intelligence Briefing · 2020–2024

Ireland's housing crisis is a supply problem wearing a price tag.

280,125 real transactions. 26 counties. Five years of data from the official Property Price Register. What follows is not opinion. It is the evidence, drawn from the government's own figures.

01 / THE AFFORDABILITY COLLAPSE

Dublin now costs a buyer 10.2 years of gross salary.

In 2020, buying in Dublin required 7.4 years of the average annual wage. By 2024 that figure had reached 10.2x. At Irish macro-prudential limits of LTI 4x and LTV 90%, a first-time buyer's maximum affordable price in Dublin is €266,000. The median is €495,000. The 46.7% affordability gap is structural, not cyclical, and it cannot be closed by income growth at the current rate of price appreciation.

02 / THE SUPPLY DEFICIT

No county built more than 73% of what it needed.

National supply deficit: 47,000+ units in 2024 alone. Dublin's completions cover just 48% of estimated demand. The cumulative shortfall since 2020 exceeds 210,000 units. Supply constraint is the mechanism driving prices, not demand. Until completions consistently exceed demand, upward price pressure in high-deficit counties is near-permanent.

03 / THE CAPITAL PREMIUM

Dublin is not the Irish market. It is a market within it.

Dublin's 2024 median of €495,000 sits 49% above the Rest-of-Ireland figure of €332,000. That premium was 38% in 2020. It is widening. Within Dublin, D06W carries a median of €825,000 against D24 at €270,000 — a €555,000 spread inside a single county. A single national rate, or even a single Dublin rate, is wrong by construction in this market.

04 / THE INVESTMENT SIGNAL

The midlands are outgrowing Dublin on every metric that matters for returns.

Leitrim, Roscommon, Longford, Cavan, and Monaghan posted four-year price growth of 35–44% from a low base. New-build premiums of 60–83% in these counties signal genuine local supply scarcity. Monaghan and Cavan are leading the country on 3-month price momentum, above 23%. At LTI-constrained prices, these markets offer yield, growth, and a structural supply argument that Dublin cannot match.

05 / THE TIMING SIGNAL

The market has a seasonal pattern consistent across all five years.

October to December delivers a price index of 103–104 with transaction volume 44% above the annual average. It is the structurally optimal window to sell. January to May produces a price index of 96–98 with the lowest buyer competition of the year. Five years of the same pattern is not noise. It is a reliable signal.

Executive Takeaway

Irish property increasingly rewards precision over scale. The national median is a useful benchmark. It is a poor basis for any decision that involves a specific county, postcode, or purchase window.

The structural signals
  • Supply deficit is the primary price driver, not demand
  • Dublin affordability gap is structural and widening
  • Midlands counties offer the best risk-adjusted returns
  • Seasonal patterns are consistent and actionable
Where the data breaks convention
  • New build premium is highest where supply is actually scarce
  • Top investment counties are not the most expensive counties
  • Dublin postcode spreads make county-level data misleading
  • Volume leads price: transaction velocity is a leading indicator
