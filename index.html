If you only read one thing
What this means for you
The same data lands differently depending on which side of the market you are on. Here is the one-line verdict for each.
🔑For buyers
Affordability has fallen faster than wages can recover.
A Dublin home now costs 10.2 years of gross salary, up from 7.4 in 2020. At Central Bank lending limits, a first-time buyer can afford roughly half the Dublin median. Wage growth of 3–5% a year cannot close a gap that is widening faster than that.
Verdict: priced out of the capital
📈For investors
The signal has moved to the commuter belt and midlands.
Structural under-supply keeps prices firm everywhere, but the strongest fundamentals are now in Meath, Kildare, and the midlands: lower entry prices, high new-build premiums, and 35–44% four-year growth from a low base. Dublin is mature; the growth is elsewhere.
Verdict: look past Dublin
🏠For renters
There is no slack left in the system.
Under 1,800 homes were available to rent nationwide in early 2026, the lowest on record. A single average earner spends 40% of gross pay on a Dublin one-bed. For most under-30s, a room, not a home, is the realistic unit, and even room rents are now rising fastest.
Verdict: structurally squeezed
🏛️For policymakers
No county built enough to meet its own demand.
Completions covered only a fraction of estimated need in 2024, leaving a deficit on the order of 47,000 homes. The shortfall, not buyer behaviour, is what sustains prices and rents. Supply is the binding constraint, and subsidies that lift demand without lifting supply make it worse.
Verdict: supply or nothing

01 / THE AFFORDABILITY COLLAPSE

Dublin now costs a buyer 10.2 years of gross salary.

In 2020, buying in Dublin required 7.4 years of the average annual wage. By 2024 that figure had reached 10.2x. At Irish macro-prudential limits of LTI 4x and LTV 90%, a first-time buyer's maximum affordable price in Dublin is €266,000. The median is €495,000. The 46.7% affordability gap is structural, not cyclical, and it cannot be closed by income growth at the current rate of price appreciation.

02 / THE SUPPLY DEFICIT

No county built more than 73% of what it needed.

National supply deficit: 47,000+ units in 2024 alone. Dublin's completions cover just 48% of estimated demand. The cumulative shortfall since 2020 exceeds 210,000 units. Supply constraint is the mechanism driving prices, not demand. Until completions consistently exceed demand, upward price pressure in high-deficit counties is near-permanent.

03 / THE CAPITAL PREMIUM

Dublin is not the Irish market. It is a market within it.

Dublin's 2024 median of €495,000 sits 49% above the Rest-of-Ireland figure of €332,000. That premium was 38% in 2020. It is widening. Within Dublin, D06W carries a median of €825,000 against D24 at €270,000, a €555,000 spread inside a single county. A single national rate, or even a single Dublin rate, is wrong by construction in this market.

03.5 / THE OTHER HALF OF THE CRISIS

If you can't buy, you rent. That market is worse.

The buy-side story is about who gets locked out. The rent-side story is about what happens to everyone else. By the end of 2025, the average two-bed nationwide reached €2,086 a month, market rents had risen for 13 of the last 14 years, and fewer than 1,800 homes were available to rent across the entire country on the first of February. For most working people and nearly every student, renting is now the only option, and the only option is broken.

Dublin · critical
The capital is the epicentre.
Dublin's average apartment rent has climbed to nearly €2,700 a month, while the number of homes available to rent fell by over one third in a single year. The RTB index of registered tenancies, which includes older below-market leases, still puts the Dublin average at €1,744 for a one-bed, €2,253 for a two-bed, and €2,585 for a three-bed. New-to-market asking rents run 20–40% higher than that. A renter on the average wage now hands over the majority of take-home pay before a single bill is paid.

Whole of Ireland
The squeeze has spread everywhere.
For years Dublin was the outlier. No longer. In 2025 the other cities saw the sharpest rises: Galway up 11.4%, Cork 7.5%, Waterford 6.9%, Limerick 5%. Outside Leinster, the stock of rental homes has collapsed to around a quarter of pre-pandemic levels. The crisis has re-synchronised across the country, not because Dublin improved, but because everywhere else caught up.

How people actually cope
Renting, sharing, and just about surviving
When whole homes vanish, people don't stop needing somewhere to live. They subdivide. The room has become the unit of the Irish rental market.
🚪
Sharing is the default
≈1 : 1
In Dublin, the ratio of room rentals to whole-property rentals has hovered around one for a decade, and at peak pressure rooms made up more than half of all listings. Room rents jumped 4.9% in 2025, far faster than the 1% of prior years. A room, not a flat, is what most renters are actually competing for.
💶
What a room costs
€400–€850
A shared room can start near €400 a month; a private room in a shared apartment runs €850 and up, higher in Dublin. At the extreme, short-let landlords have advertised single rooms in shared houses for over €7,000 a month, a sign of how far demand has outrun supply.
🎓
Students hit hardest
€5.7k–€11.9k
On-campus rooms run from €5,722 to €11,888 per academic year, the dearest at UCD. Demand for student beds in Dublin is projected past 115,000 by 2034/35, with supply far behind. Many students commute hours daily, defer a year, or leave Ireland to study abroad because they cannot find a bed.

04 / THE INVESTMENT SIGNAL

The midlands are outgrowing Dublin on every metric that matters for returns.

Leitrim, Roscommon, Longford, Cavan, and Monaghan posted four-year price growth of 35–44% from a low base. New-build premiums of 60–83% in these counties signal genuine local supply scarcity. Monaghan and Cavan are leading the country on 3-month price momentum, above 23%. At LTI-constrained prices, these markets offer yield, growth, and a structural supply argument that Dublin cannot match.

05 / THE TIMING SIGNAL

The market has a seasonal pattern consistent across all five years.

October to December delivers a price index of 103–104 with transaction volume 44% above the annual average. It is the structurally optimal window to sell. January to May produces a price index of 96–98 with the lowest buyer competition of the year. Five years of the same pattern is not noise. It is a reliable signal.

Executive Takeaway

Irish property increasingly rewards precision over scale. The national median is a useful benchmark. It is a poor basis for any decision that involves a specific county, postcode, or purchase window.

The structural signals
  • Supply deficit is the primary price driver, not demand
  • Dublin affordability gap is structural and widening
  • Midlands counties offer the best risk-adjusted returns
  • Seasonal patterns are consistent and actionable
Where the data breaks convention
  • New build premium is highest where supply is actually scarce
  • Top investment counties are not the most expensive counties
  • Dublin postcode spreads make county-level data misleading
  • Volume leads price: transaction velocity is a leading indicator
What to do about it
Strategic recommendations
The same evidence reads differently depending on who is holding it. Four positions, four plays.
For first-time buyers
Look past the commuter belt, not just beyond the M50.
Kildare, Meath, and Wicklow carry Dublin's supply pressure at marginally better prices. The genuine affordability and growth combination sits further out, in the midlands. Time the purchase for January to May, when prices run 3 to 4% below the annual average.
Implication: widen the search radius, narrow the buying window.
For property investors
The yield is in the counties the headlines ignore.
Leitrim, Roscommon, Longford, Cavan, and Monaghan combine 35 to 44% four-year growth with new-build premiums of 60 to 83%, a clear signal of local scarcity. All five score Tier 1 on the composite model. Dublin sits in Tier 2.
Implication: midlands exposure beats capital exposure on risk-adjusted return.
For mortgage lenders
Affordability stress is structural, not a cycle to wait out.
At LTI 4x and LTV 90%, the Dublin median is 46.7% beyond reach for a median earner. This gap widened every year from 2020. Underwriting models calibrated to a national median will misprice risk at postcode level.
Implication: price risk at postcode granularity, not county averages.
For policymakers
Demand-side intervention cannot fix a supply problem.
No county delivered more than 73% of estimated demand in 2024. The cumulative deficit since 2020 exceeds 210,000 units. Buyer subsidies into a fixed-supply market transfer to price, not to access.
Implication: completions, not subsidies, are the binding constraint.

How the numbers were built
Methodology & models
Every figure on this page is reproducible. These are the definitions, formulae, and data-handling decisions behind the headline claims, stated plainly so they can be checked.
Data pipeline
From 314k raw rows to 280,125 clean transactions
Raw PPR exports were de-duplicated, VAT-adjusted on new builds, price-parsed from mixed string formats, and filtered for valid date and county fields. Roughly 11% of rows were dropped as duplicates, nulls, or non-market transfers.
Ingest→ Parse price→ Dedupe→ VAT adjust→ Median by county/yr
Affordability model
Years-of-salary and the 46.7% gap
Affordability ratio is county median price divided by CSO mean annual earnings. The first-time-buyer gap compares the regulatory maximum loan against the actual median.
years_to_buy = median_price ÷ gross_annual_wage
ftb_gap = 1 − (income×4 + deposit) ÷ median_price
Composite investment score
A four-factor, weight-normalised index (0–100)
Each county's growth, yield proxy, supply scarcity, and affordability are min-max scaled to 0–1, then combined on fixed weights. Affordability stress enters as a penalty, which is why Dublin ranks Tier 2 despite strong growth.
4yr growth 40% Yield proxy 25% Supply scarcity 20% Affordability 15%
Supply–demand & seasonality
Deficit estimation and the monthly index
Demand is modelled from household-formation and obsolescence estimates per county; the supply ratio is completions ÷ estimated demand. Seasonality uses a monthly index where 100 is the annual mean, derived across all five years.
supply_ratio = completions ÷ est_demand
season_index = month_avg ÷ annual_avg × 100
Limitations & honesty note. The PPR records sale price but not floor area or condition, so medians are not quality-adjusted. Demand estimates and the yield proxy are modelled, not measured. New-build VAT treatment introduces minor noise. Figures are decision-support signals, not appraisals, and that distinction is stated deliberately.
